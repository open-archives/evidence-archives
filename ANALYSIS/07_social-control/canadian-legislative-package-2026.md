## Bill C-8: Critical Cyber Systems Protection Act (CCSPA)

### What It Does
| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Telecommunications Act amendments** | New order-making powers for Governor-in-Council and Minister of Industry to direct telcos to take specific actions |
| **New cybersecurity regime** | Designated operators of "vital services and systems" subject to compliance obligations |
| **Sectors covered** | Banking, transportation, energy, telecommunications |
| **Security orders** | Minister can issue orders affecting telecom infrastructure |
| **Data stewardship** | Personal information collected under security orders must be disposed of when no longer required |

### Concerns
| Issue | Source |
|-------|--------|
| **Encryption risk** | Citizen Lab warned new ministerial powers could be used to compromise network security |
| **Privacy impacts** | Privacy Commissioner recommendations partially adopted but gaps remain |
| **Scope creep** | "Designated operator" definition broad—could expand to smaller businesses over time |
| **Ministerial discretion** | Orders issued without parliamentary oversight |

**What the Government Says**
> "Strengthening the protection of Canada's critical cyber systems."

**What Critics Say**
Citizen Lab (May 2026 brief) recommended:
> "Section 15.2(2.1) should be clarified to confirm protection for encryption and technical safeguards in telecommunication networks generally, not just the specific encryption that is attached to private communications."
*Translation:* The government resisted explicit encryption protections in the bill text.

## Bill C-9: Combatting Hate Act

### What It Does
| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Hate symbol ban** | Criminalizes displaying Nazi symbols (two specified), terrorist entity symbols, and nooses in public |
| **Intimidation offence** | Criminalizes intimidating or obstructing access to places of worship, schools, community centres |
| **Exclusion zones** | Creates zones where protest can be restricted |
| **Religious defense removed** | Eliminates longstanding Criminal Code defense protecting good-faith religious arguments |
| **Attorney General consent** | Re-introduced requirement for AG consent before charges (procedural safeguard) |
| **Definition of hatred** | Supreme Court standard: "extreme detestation or vilification" |

## Bill C-22: Lawful Access Act

### What It Does
| Provision | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Production orders** | Judges can order telecom providers to hand over transmission data and subscriber information |
| **Threshold lowered** | "Reasonable grounds to suspect"—the lowest investigative threshold in Canadian criminal law |
| **Previous standard** | "Reasonable grounds to believe"—higher bar that governed production orders for past decade |
| **Confirmation of service demands** | Police can demand telecom providers confirm if they have information relevant to investigation |
| **Foreign telecoms** | Minister of Justice can authorize foreign entities to compel production of data held in Canada |
| **Surveillance obligations** | Electronic service providers may be required to build technical capabilities they don't currently have |
| **Mutual Legal Assistance** | Amends MLAC to allow enforcement of foreign data production orders |

**Status:** Still pending as of July 2026—moving through legislative process.

### The Signal Threat
Signal (encrypted messaging app) warned it would pull out of the Canadian market entirely rather than comply with C-22's surveillance obligations. This means:
- If C-22 passes as written
- Signal cannot legally operate in Canada
- Canadians lose access to one of the most secure communication tools available
- Only large tech companies with resources to comply remain

### Apple and Google Warnings
**Apple (Director of User Privacy, Erik Neuenschwander):**
> "When you build a backdoor into an encrypted device, anyone can walk through and cause so much [harm]... so much is built on encryption we can't take that risk."

**Google:** Warned Bill C-22 could create cybersecurity risks.

**EFF (Electronic Frontier Foundation):**
> "Canada is forging ahead with its dangerous surveillance bill."
*Key criticism:* Government prevented Part 2 (surveillance obligations) from being independently debated.

**Canadian Civil Liberties Association (CCLA):**
