## Executive Summary

The term **"catch and release"** refers to a pattern where individuals — often repeat or violent offenders — are arrested, processed, and released back into the community within hours or days, sometimes committing new crimes while awaiting trial. Driven by a bail system favoring the "principle of restraint," Canada saw a **43.8%** increase in violent crime between 2010 and 2022 (compared to a 14% drop in the U.S.).

This growing public safety crisis eroded trust in the justice system and led to the passage of **Bill C-14 (The Bail and Sentencing Reform Act)** on July 15, 2026. The legislation introduced sweeping "reverse onus" provisions for repeat and violent offenders, shifting the burden of proof from the Crown to the accused. While supported by police associations and municipalities, critics argue it undermines the presumption of innocence and disproportionately impacts marginalized communities, particularly Indigenous peoples.

## Part 2: The Legal Framework Before Reform

### The "Restraint Principle"
Canadian law presumes that individuals are eligible for bail. Historically, the Crown (prosecution) bore the burden of proving why a person should be detained. Judges were required to apply the **"principle of restraint"** — releasing accused persons at the earliest opportunity with the least restrictive conditions possible. Critics argue this severely limited the ability to detain repeat violent offenders.

### The Gladue Principle
Judges must consider the unique systemic and historical circumstances of Indigenous offenders (Gladue principles). While intended to address the systemic over-incarceration of Indigenous peoples, critics argue it was applied too broadly, sometimes resulting in leniency for repeat violent offenders.

## Part 3: The 2026 Reform — Bill C-14

Passed on **July 15, 2026**, Bill C-14 introduced multiple **"reverse onus"** provisions. Under reverse onus, the accused must prove to a judge why they should be released, rather than the Crown proving why they should be detained.

### Sentencing Changes
| Provision | Change |
|-----------|--------|
| **Consecutive sentences** | Courts can impose consecutive sentences for violent car theft, home invasion, and other serious offenses |
| **Restricted conditional sentences** | Conditional sentences (house arrest) now restricted for serious offenses |

## Part 4: Support vs. Criticism

### Support
- **Canadian Police Association (CPA)**: Called it a "victory for public safety"
- **National Police Federation (NPF)**: Representing 20,000 RCMP members
- **Municipal Leaders**: E.g., Brampton Mayor Patrick Brown ("restoring accountability")
- **Halton Police Chief**: Called it a "pendulum swing toward victims"
- **Union of B.C. Municipalities**: Passed a resolution urging reform

### Concerns & Criticism
- **Criminal defense lawyers**: Argue reverse onus violates Charter rights (presumption of innocence)
- **Indigenous organizations**: Argue it will disproportionately impact Indigenous and Black communities (Indigenous peoples make up ~5% of population but >30% of federal prison population)
- **Prison reform advocates**: Cite worsening overcrowding and underfunded rehabilitation
- **Criminologists**: Argue reform won't reduce crime because social issues (poverty, addiction) remain unaddressed

## Part 5: Before vs. After Bill C-14

| Element | Before (Pre-C-14) | After (C-14) |
|---------|-------------------|--------------|
| **Bail burden** | Crown must prove detention | Accused must prove release (for designated offenses) |
| **Repeat offenses** | No reverse onus | Reverse onus for third violent offense |
| **Violent crimes (carjacking, home invasion, strangulation)** | Standard bail rules | Reverse onus applies |
| **Consecutive sentencing** | Limited | Expanded for violent offenses |
| **Conditional sentences (house arrest)** | Available for many offenses | Restricted for serious offenses |
