## Part 1: Funding Structure & Dependencies

### Revenue Breakdown (2026-27 Budget)

| Source | Percentage | Amount | Notes |
|--------|------------|--------|-------|
| **Assessed Contributions** | 50% target by 2030-31 | ~$2.1B of $4.2B total | Member state dues, 20% increase approved May 2025 |
| **Voluntary Contributions** | 50%+ of current budget | ~$2.1B | Unpredictable, donor-directed |
| **Bill & Melinda Gates Foundation** | 9.5% of revenues | $5.5B (2000-2024) | 640 grants, 2nd largest donor before US withdrawal |
| **WHO Foundation** | Growing portion | $83M collected through 2023 | 80% anonymous in 2023 (up from 15% Year 1) |
| **United States** | Previously largest | Dropped to 7th place in 2024 | Withdrawal completed Jan 2026 per Trump EO |

### Focal Point Clarification: Two Funding Streams

#### Member-State Dues (Assessed Contributions) ✅ Transparent
| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| **Source** | 193 member states (post-US: 192) |
| **Calculation** | Based on GDP + population formula |
| **Control** | Collective member state voting through World Health Assembly |
| **Usage** | WHO strategic priorities (GPW 14: 2025-2028) |
| **Transparency** | Public reporting, audited financial statements |
| **Percentage** | ~50% of core budget (targeting 50% by 2030-31) |

#### Voluntary Earmarked Contributions ⚠️ Mission-Directed Funding
| Characteristic | Details |
|----------------|---------|
| **Source** | Individual governments, Gates Foundation, corporate partnerships, WHO Foundation |
| **Control** | Donor specifies program priorities ("earmarked") |
| **Usage** | Donor-selected initiatives, not necessarily WHO strategic plan |
| **Transparency** | Limited—WHO Foundation donor anonymity up to 80% (2023) |
| **Percentage** | ~50%+ of total funding |
| **Conflict Risk** | PRIMARY MECHANISM through which corporate/philanthropic influence bypasses WHO strategic goals |

**Why This Distinction Matters:**
Traditional UN model: Member states collectively determine priorities through democratic governance.
WHO's hybrid model: Half-funded by donors who dictate spending priorities, effectively:
- Allowing Gates Foundation to prioritize vaccination over maternal health
- Enabling pharma partnerships to focus on specific disease areas
- Creating donor competition for WHO attention/resources
- Undermining WHO's autonomy to set agenda based on global health needs

**Key Funding Concerns:**
- **Voluntary vs. Core Funding:** Only ~50% of budget comes from assessed contributions. Remaining 50%+ is voluntary—meaning donors specify how money should be spent.
- **Gates Foundation Dominance:** With $5.5B contributed 2000-2024 and 9.5% of total revenues, giving a private foundation outsized influence on global health priorities.
- **"Dark Money" Growth:** WHO Foundation anonymity increased dramatically (15% in Year 1 to 80% in 2023). Total anonymous: ~$60M of $83M.

## Part 1A: US Withdrawal Fallout & Governance Fragmentation

### Timeline
| Date | Event | Impact |
|------|-------|--------|
| **Jan 20, 2025** | Trump signs EO to withdraw from WHO | Process initiated |
| **Jan 22, 2026** | Withdrawal completed (78-year membership ends) | 18% of core funding lost |
| **May 2025** | Pandemic Agreement adopted | US absent from final negotiations |
| **2025-2026** | G7 accelerates minilateral coalitions | Governance decentralization begins |

### Funding Loss Details
| Component | Amount | Consequence |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| **Core assessed contribution** | ~18% of WHO budget | $750-800M annual shortfall |
| **Voluntary US programs** | Additional millions | Polio eradication, HIV/AIDS, malaria initiatives disrupted |
| **Technical capacity** | CDC collaboration reduced | Disease surveillance weakened |
| **Outstanding debt** | >$130M owed | Complicates future re-entry negotiations |

### Minilateral Fragmentation
Instead of strengthening WHO, US withdrawal has catalyzed parallel governance structures:

| Coalition | Structure | Function | Relationship to WHO |
|-----------|-----------|----------|---------------------|
| **G7 Health Ministers' Forum** | Issue-specific alliance | Data sharing, vaccine coordination, AMR monitoring | Operates outside UN consensus |
| **G7 Pandemic Preparedness Hub** | New infrastructure | Joint vaccine development, rapid response deployment | Complementary/substitute to WHO mechanisms |
| **EU Centre for Disease Prevention** | Regional body | Disease surveillance for EU members | More influential without US participation |
| **Emerging "One Health" Coalitions** | Ad-hoc partnerships | Zoonotic disease tracking | Fragmented vs. integrated approach |

### Governance Implications
Before 2025: WHO served as central coordinating body for global health governance, with 194 member states operating under consensus-based decision-making.
After 2026: Three-tier structure emerges:
1. Core WHO (weakened, underfunded, 193 remaining members)
2. Minilateral clubs (G7, EU-led, donor-driven) making decisions outside WHO
3. Bilateral arrangements (individual country partnerships)

### Strategic Consequences
- **Disease surveillance gaps:** US scientists lose early-warning data access from other countries
- **Vaccine strain selection:** Decentralized coordination may reduce accuracy/equity
- **Equity erosion:** Wealthier nations form exclusive partnerships, LMICs left behind
- **WHO marginalization:** Organization becomes reactive rather than proactive
- **Accountability diffusion:** Multiple bodies, unclear responsibility for failures

> **Lawrence Gostin (Georgetown):** "Withdrawal will hurt global response to new outbreaks and will hobble the ability of U.S. scientists and pharmaceutical companies to develop vaccines and medicines against new threats."

**Problem:** WHO accepts donations from company that keeps essential medicine prices high, while WHO advocates for affordability in low-income countries.

### Documented Cases (2025-2026)
Per internal WHO review (late 2025):

| Official | Previous WHO Role | New Pharma Position | Timeframe |
|----------|-------------------|---------------------|-----------|
| **Former Vaccine Procurement Head** | WHO vaccine purchasing | Sanofi executive | Within 12 months |
| **Director, Antimicrobial Resistance** | WHO AMR program lead | GSK consultancy | Within 12 months |
| **Director, Global Drug Facility** | WHO drug procurement | Novartis position | Within 12 months |
| **Additional officials** | Various senior roles | Pfizer, Merck positions | Within 12 months |

*Total tracked: At least 15 ex-WHO officials joined pharma sector by early 2026.*

### Policy Change (February 2026)
In response to scrutiny, WHO adopted:
- **Extended cool-off period:** 6 months → 18 months mandatory waiting period
- **Full disclosure requirement:** Post-employment arrangements with pharma must be disclosed
- **Scope:** Applies to departing senior employees

*Problem:* New policy applies going forward—doesn't address past employment or create enforcement mechanisms.

### The "Shadow Workforce" Problem

**Formal Staff Cuts vs. Unreported Consultants**
| Metric | Formal Staff | Consultants/Contingent |
|--------|--------------|------------------------|
| **December 2024** | 9,457 | ~3,500-4,000 (estimated) |
| **Mid-2026 Projection** | 7,086 (-25%) | ~3,500-4,000 (steady) |
| **Net Impact** | Lost 2,371 positions | Same workforce size, less accountability |

**Critical Finding:** WHO reports cutting 25% of staff by mid-2026, but this only counts formal employees. The "shadow workforce" of consultants remains largely unreported.

**Why This Matters**
| Issue | Impact |
|-------|--------|
| **Transparency bypass** | Consultants not subject to same recruitment protocols, conflict disclosures, or employment vetting |
| **Secondment arrangements** | Personnel from NGOs, private institutions, member states work at WHO without permanent employment records |
| **Accountability gap** | No clear chain of command, easier to dismiss, fewer oversight protections |
| **Continuity risk** | High turnover undermines institutional memory, policy consistency |
| **Cost opacity** | Consultant fees not captured in standard staff budget lines |

Source: Health Policy Watch investigation (October 2025)—report provides "no data as to the number of non-staff consultants who continue to be employed."

**Power Dynamic Shift**
```
Formal WHO Staff (Reduced 25%) → Lower Accountability
         ↓
Consultants/Seconded Staff (~3,500-4,000) → Higher Flexibility, Less Oversight
         ↓
Result: Core functions maintained, but through less accountable channels
```
*Who Benefits?:* Partner NGOs, private institutions gain indirect access to WHO operations and decision-making processes without transparent employment records.

## Part 4: Pandemic Treaty Negotiations & One Health (2021-2026)

### Process Transparency Issues
| Issue | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Closed-door sessions** | Key drafting not publicly streamed or documented in real-time |
| **Limited civil society input** | Academics, NGOs, public marginalized in negotiation process |
| **Industry dominance** | Pharmaceutical lobbyists shaped PABS (Pandemic Access and Benefit Sharing) System text |
| **LMIC exclusion** | Low/middle-income nations' voices on equitable access weakened |
| **Final deal failure** | May 2026: Couldn't agree on final part—Independent Panel called it "deeply regrettable" |

### PABS System Controversy
What it is: Pandemic Access and Benefit Sharing framework for vaccine/therapeutic distribution
**What pharma wanted:**
- Intellectual property protections maintained
- Voluntary participation (not mandatory)
- 20% production commitment to WHO during pandemics (instead of more)

**What LMICs wanted:**
- Mandatory tech transfer
- Stronger equity provisions
- Binding production commitments

**Result:** Final text (May 2025 adoption) leaned toward pharma preferences—PABS remains voluntary with limited IP reforms.

### "One Health" Definition Battle in PABS Negotiations

**The Sticking Point:**
During Pandemic Agreement negotiations, the definition of "pathogen" became a critical flashpoint:
| Position | Definition | Rationale |
|----------|------------|-----------|
| **LMICs + Advocacy Groups** | Zoonotic + animal-origin pathogens (broader scope) | 75% of emerging infectious diseases originate in animals—need inclusion for benefit-sharing |
| **Pharma Industry + Some Member States** | Human pathogens only (narrower scope) | Limit complexity of IP negotiations, reduce liability exposure |

**What's at Stake:**
- *If narrow definition prevails:* Animal-origin threats excluded from PABS, LMICs lose leverage to access vaccines, 75% of threats fall outside framework.
- *If broad definition prevails:* Technology-transfer hubs cover animal-origin diseases, greater equity, broader IP licensing requirements for pharma.

**Current Status (May 2026):**
The adopted treaty explicitly integrates One Health approach and defines pathogen to include "zoonotic biological agent...that originates in animals." However, the final part of the accord remained stalled as of May 2026. *Implication: While the treaty text appears to favor broader definition, implementation details and PABS Annex specifics remain contested.*

## Part 7: Transparency Assessment

### What WHO Publishes ✅
- Member state contributions (Public database)
- Budget documents (Available online)
- Pandemic Agreement text (Full text published)
- Staff employment data (Aggregated numbers only)
- Board meeting summaries (Partial transcripts)

### What WHO Does NOT Disclose ❌
- WHO Foundation donor names ("Anonymous" option for privacy)
- Corporate partnership meeting minutes (Privileged discussions)
- Individual staff conflict declarations (Privacy protections)
- Internal investigation details (Ongoing cases)
- Pharmaceutical negotiation drafts (Confidential until final text)

### 990/Financial Disclosure Gap
Unlike US 501(c)(3) nonprofits, WHO (UN specialized agency):
- No requirement to name individual donors above thresholds
- Swiss-based foundation (WHO Foundation) operates under Swiss privacy law
- Corporate partnership details aggregated, not itemized
*Result: Cannot trace specific company donations to specific policy outcomes.*
