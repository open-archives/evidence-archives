## Executive Summary

The Foundation for Defense of Democracies (FDD) is a Washington, D.C.-based think tank that has positioned itself as a nonpartisan analyst of national security issues. Founded in 2001 by Clifford May, the organization has become a key ideological engine for shaping U.S. Middle East policy, particularly regarding Iran.

Critics argue that FDD "functions less as an independent think tank and more as an ideological extension of Israeli security doctrine." Its network includes former Israeli military and intelligence officials who have spent years pushing the U.S. toward confrontation with Iran. With an annual budget of approximately $10 million and access to senior U.S. policymakers, FDD exerts outsized influence relative to its size.

**FDD's self-description:** The organization presents itself as a nonpartisan national security think tank focused on counter-terrorism, Iran threats, and democratic allies. It argues its positions are driven by U.S. interests against jihadist and Iranian threats — not by loyalty to any foreign power.

### FDD's Official Position on the Revolving Door

FDD maintains that its employees adhere to a strict "revolving door policy" that prioritizes national security over personal gain. The organization does not directly name specific officials but has publicly stated its commitment to "prevent its experts from using their positions at the organization as a stepping stone for private sector or government roles."

### Revolving Door Nuance

This is not unique to FDD. The revolving door is a standard feature of Washington's influence ecosystem. Every major think tank — from Brookings on the left to Heritage on the right — sees its staff rotate through government positions. It is a feature of the system, not a unique corruption of FDD.
