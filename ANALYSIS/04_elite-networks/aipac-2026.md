## Executive Summary

The American Israel Public Affairs Committee (AIPAC) is the most powerful and well-funded pro-Israel lobbying organization in the United States. In the 2026 election cycle, it has become a dominant force in campaign spending — outspending many other major lobbying groups. Through its network of super PACs and affiliated groups, AIPAC has reshaped congressional races from North Carolina to Illinois, while triggering a backlash that would have been unthinkable just two years ago.

However, AIPAC's influence is not unique in American politics. It operates alongside other powerful lobbying forces — the NRA, pharmaceutical lobbies, unions, Saudi/UAE efforts, and Big Tech. Foreign policy lobbies in general punch above their weight in Washington, and AIPAC is the most successful among them.

This entry documents both AIPAC's power and its limits, acknowledging that its success also reflects alignment with broader U.S. strategic interests, genuine voter constituencies, and bipartisan support that predates its current spending levels.

## Part 1: History — From Zionist Emergency Council to AIPAC

| Year | Event | Significance |
|------|-------|--------------|
| **1942** | American Zionist Emergency Council (AZEC) formed | Advocated for Jewish immigration to Palestine and creation of a Jewish state |
| **1949** | AZEC succeeded by American Zionist Council (AZC) | Lobbied for federal aid to Israel |
| **1953** | **Qibya massacre** — IDF raid kills 66 Arab villagers; U.S. considers forcing AZC to register as foreign agent | Eisenhower administration suspects Israeli government funding AZC |
| **1954** | **AZCPA founded** (Isaiah Kenen) | Direct response to Qibya condemnation; run on non-tax-deductible donations to avoid lobbying restrictions |
| **1959** | **Name changed to AIPAC** | Broadened appeal to include mainstream Jewish organizations |
| **1974** | Morris Amitay takes over | Transformed AIPAC from "intimate, low-budget operation into a large, mass-based organization" |
| **2021** | AIPAC PAC founded | Largest PAC contributor on dollar-for-dollar basis |
| **2024** | AIPAC PAC contributes $53M+ to 361 candidates | 96% of supported candidates won |

## Part 4: Shifting Strategy — From Open Warfare to Quiet Influence

AIPAC spent over $100 million in the 2024 elections to oust critics of Israel. That success met with "public disgust" with Israel's war in Gaza and fueled a growing movement to "eradicate AIPAC's influence."

In 2026, the group has "pulled back from the aggressive electoral strategy they pursued last time." Former Rep. Marie Newman (D-IL) told The Intercept: *"They are fully aware their brand is in the toilet."*

Despite the quieter public profile, AIPAC continues working behind closed doors to boost preferred candidates.

**Effectiveness note:** Despite the backlash, AIPAC's success rate with endorsed candidates remains very high, and many mainstream Democrats continue to welcome its support. The shift is tactical, not a sign of declining power.

**Quote from Omar Shakir, DAWN Executive Director:**
> *"AIPAC shapes US policy in the Middle East but operates entirely in the shadows. AIPAC's tax-exempt status means that American taxpayers effectively subsidize the pro-Israel lobby and they deserve to know how AIPAC works and who works for it."*

### Legal Status

AIPAC is a **domestic lobby of American citizens** — not a foreign agent. It is not required to register under the Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA). This distinction is legally significant and is a core point of contention between critics and defenders. Critics argue it should be subject to FARA; defenders maintain it is an American organization representing American citizens.

## Part 9: Comparative Context — AIPAC vs. Other Lobbies

AIPAC is powerful, but it is not unique. It operates alongside other major lobbying forces:

| Lobby | Estimated Annual Spending | Notes |
|-------|---------------------------|-------|
| **Pharmaceutical industry** | ~$300M+ | One of the largest lobbying forces |
| **NRA** | ~$50M+ | Declining, but still significant |
| **AIPAC / UDP** | ~$100M+ | 2026 cycle; rising |
| **Big Tech (Google, Amazon, etc.)** | ~$200M+ | Combined lobbying and PACs |
| **Saudi Arabia (foreign lobby)** | ~$20M+ | FARA-registered |
| **UAE (foreign lobby)** | ~$15M+ | FARA-registered |

**Note:** Foreign lobbies (Saudi, UAE) must register under FARA. AIPAC does not. This is a key distinction in the debate over its influence.

**AIPAC is powerful, but it is not unique.** Foreign policy lobbies in general punch above their weight in Washington.
