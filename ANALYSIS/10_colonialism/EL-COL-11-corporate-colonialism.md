## Executive Summary

Corporate colonialism is not a metaphor. It is a historical and ongoing system in which private corporations—granted state-like powers through royal charters—acted as the vanguard of European imperial expansion, and continue to shape the political economy of the Global South today.

The Dutch East India Company (VOC) and its British counterpart were not merely commercial enterprises; they were "states within states," granted the power to wage war, coin money, negotiate treaties, and establish colonies. As historian William Dalrymple documented, it was not the British government that seized India at the end of the 18th century, but "a dangerously unregulated private company headquartered in one small office, five windows wide, in London, and managed in India by an unstable sociopath." This corporate entity used private armies, economic coercion, and political manipulation to pillage nations under the guise of trade.

In Africa, the pattern was the same. Chartered companies—the Imperial British East African Company, the British South Africa Company, the Royal Niger Company, the German East Africa Company—financed the administration of the countries in which they operated on behalf of their home governments, signed treaties with local leaders, created security organs, and laid the infrastructure that facilitated colonial administrative policies. They were the "corpolonialism" described by novelist Ngugi wa Thiong'o—a fusion of corporate and colonial power that extracted wealth from the continent for centuries.

The 1926 Firestone concession in Liberia exemplifies the model: a 99-year lease over one million acres, with the Liberian government receiving only $8 million in revenue while Firestone exported $160 million worth of rubber between 1940 and 1965. The company tied its concession to a $5 million government loan, ensuring the U.S. government would protect its interests. Liberia was, as Walter Rodney observed, "an American colony in everything but name."

This entry examines the historical evolution of corporate colonialism—from the chartered companies of the 17th–19th centuries to the "Banana Republics" of Latin America (United Fruit), the oil companies in Nigeria and Iran (Shell, BP), and the contemporary "corporate lawfare" that enables neocolonial resource extraction. It argues that the corporation-state nexus is not a historical artifact but a living system that continues to shape Global South political economies, often more powerfully than formal colonial administration ever did.

The entry concludes with an assessment of the 2026 moment: the return of corporate colonialism rhetoric in U.S. foreign policy, the EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive, and the ongoing struggle for African sovereignty in the face of corporate plunder.

## Part 1: The Chartered Company System — States Within States

### 1.1 The Origins

The chartered company was the original vehicle of corporate colonialism. These were private enterprises granted royal charters that conferred state-like powers:

| Power | Description |
|-------|-------------|
| **Military force** | The right to raise armies, wage war, and maintain private security forces |
| **Diplomatic authority** | The right to negotiate treaties with local rulers |
| **Monetary authority** | The right to coin money |
| **Administrative authority** | The right to establish colonies, administer justice, and levy taxes |
| **Trade monopolies** | Exclusive rights to trade in designated territories |

These powers were not theoretical. The British East India Company maintained a private army that at times outnumbered the British Army. The Imperial British East African Company (IBEACO) had "its private army that comprised of Sudanese, Arabs, and Swahili and Ganda soldiers" used by the British to maintain internal stability and defeat African resistance.

### 1.2 The Dutch and British East India Companies

The Dutch East India Company (VOC) and the British East India Company were the prototypes of corporate colonialism. They were "chartered companies that were key tools for European overseas colonization and a means for European governments to 'outsource empire.'"

**Key features**:
- The VOC was granted a monopoly on Dutch trade in Asia and the power to wage war, negotiate treaties, and establish colonies.
- The British East India Company effectively governed India for nearly a century before the British Crown formally assumed control.
- Both companies engaged in the transatlantic slave trade, which "laid the basis for today's racialized global capitalism."

### 1.3 The Chartered Companies in Africa

In the late 19th century, chartered companies were "granted to several British, German and Portuguese companies" as part of the Scramble for Africa:

| Company | Territory | Role |
|---------|-----------|------|
| **British South Africa Company (BSAC)** | Zambia, Zimbabwe, Malawi | Colonized territories under Cecil Rhodes; made "dubious" claims to land and mineral rights |
| **Royal Niger Company (RNC)** | Nigeria | Signed treaties, occupied areas of influence, laid infrastructure |
| **Imperial British East African Company (IBEACO)** | Kenya, Uganda | Destroyed Arab slave trade centers; financed Uganda railway surveying |
| **German East Africa Company (GEACO)** | Tanzania | Represented German interests |
| **Companhia de Moçambique and Companhia do Niassa** | Mozambique | Portuguese experiment in governing through chartered companies (1890s–1920s) |

**The BSAC case**: The British South Africa Company, under Cecil Rhodes, made claims to "vast tracts of land and to mineral rights in the region." The problem: the company "made claims to land and minerals for which it could not show proof of any treaty with the local ruler in question." British Imperial Government officials internally labeled the claims "dubious"—but they legitimized them anyway, fearing that revoking them would damage reputations and threaten investments made by other corporations that had relied on the BSAC's claimed title.

### 1.4 The "Regulatory Capture" Model

The BSAC case exemplifies "regulatory capture"—the phenomenon in which a corporation wields such influence over government that the state serves the corporation's interests rather than the public's. As one study documents, "the British Imperial Government favored the interests of a corporation it acknowledged had no legal basis for the claims of ownership."

**The mechanism**:
1. The company made dubious claims to land and mineral rights
2. The government knew the claims were dubious
3. The government legitimized them anyway
4. The company's property rights were secured
5. African communities were dispossessed

This pattern was not unique to the BSAC. It was the operating system of corporate colonialism.

## Part 2: The "Corpolonialism" Model — Corporate Rule and State Power

### 2.1 The Concept

The late novelist and scholar Ngugi wa Thiong'o coined the term **"corpolonialism"** to describe the fusion of corporate and colonial power that has shaped African political economy for centuries. This is not a metaphor—it is a description of a system in which "corporations have exploited—butchered—the African continent, leaving behind scars, open wounds and entrails which can be seen from space."

**The unholy trinity**: White supremacy, corporate interests, and state power have been "central to the system of colonial apartheid that emerged over the course of the 20th century." As the South African Truth and Reconciliation Commission concluded: "Business was central to the economy that sustained the South African state during the apartheid years. Certain businesses…were involved in helping to design and implement apartheid policies…Most businesses benefited from operations in a racially structured context."

### 2.2 The Mechanics of Corpolonialism

| Mechanism | Description | Example |
|-----------|-------------|---------|
| **Concession agreements** | Corporations granted long-term leases over vast territories | Firestone's 99-year, one-million-acre lease in Liberia |
| **Debt as leverage** | Loans tied to concessions, ensuring government compliance | Firestone's $5 million loan tied to the 1926 agreement |
| **State protection** | Home government intervenes to protect corporate interests | U.S. government protection of Firestone in Liberia |
| **Unequal exchange** | Massive resource outflows with minimal local benefit | $160M rubber exports vs. $8M Liberian revenue (1940–1965) |
| **Regulatory capture** | State legitimizes dubious corporate claims | BSAC's property rights in Southern Rhodesia |

### 2.3 The Firestone Case: Corporate Colonialism in Liberia

Gregg Mitman's *Empire of Rubber* documents the Firestone Tire & Rubber Company's "corporate colonialism" in Liberia. In 1926, Firestone secured a 99-year lease over one million acres of Liberian land. The company tied the concession to a $5 million government loan, ensuring the U.S. government would step in to protect its interests.

**The extractive logic**:
- Firestone exported $160 million worth of rubber from Liberia between 1940 and 1965
- The Liberian government received only $8 million in revenue
- The company controlled huge swathes of Liberian land and labor

As Walter Rodney wrote in 1972, "It is common knowledge that Liberia was an American colony in everything but name." Liberia was famously "unlike other colonies"—settled by Black Americans and nominally independent—but the corporate extraction model replicated colonial patterns without formal colonial administration.

### 2.4 The Mozambique Chartered Companies: Failure and Exploitation

Portugal's experiment with chartered companies in Mozambique—the Companhia do Niassa and the Companhia de Moçambique—"proved doubly unsuccessful":

1. The companies failed to provide development capital
2. Instead, they "devoted their energies to maximizing profits through the systematic exploitation of the African populace"

Foreign governments (Britain, Germany) purchased shares in the companies and used them "as proxies to further their own imperial interests at Portugal's expense." Only with the Salazar regime in the late 1920s did Portugal feel powerful enough to terminate the experiment.

## Part 3: The Banana Republics — United Fruit and Corporate Control in Latin America

The 20th century saw the era of the "Banana Republics," whereby corporations such as United Fruit "dominated the economies and sometimes the politics of parts of Latin America." This model replicated the chartered company system without formal colonial administration.

**Key features**:
- United Fruit controlled vast landholdings in Central America
- The company dominated transportation, communication, and labor markets
- It intervened directly in politics—including the 1954 CIA-backed coup in Guatemala (covered in `EL-MONROE-04`)
- The term "Banana Republic" was coined to describe these corporate-dominated states

**The continuity**: The United Fruit Company operated as a 20th-century version of the British East India Company—a corporate entity with state-like powers, protected by its home government, extracting wealth from the Global South with minimal local benefit.

## Part 4: Oil, Minerals, and the Modern Corporate Extraction Model

### 4.1 Oil Companies as Corporate Colonial Powers

Oil companies such as BP and Royal Dutch Shell "held sway in 'key' areas such as parts of Iran and of Nigeria, despite the preservation of fictional independence." The pattern was familiar:

- Concession agreements granted corporations control over vast territories
- The home government protected corporate interests
- Local populations bore the environmental and social costs
- Profits flowed to shareholders in metropolitan countries

### 4.2 The Current Landscape: Corporate Lawfare and Neocolonial Extraction

Contemporary corporate resource extraction operates through a system of "corporate lawfare"—the use of legal mechanisms to consolidate control, suppress opposition, and neutralize local legal systems.

**Mechanisms of corporate lawfare**:

| Mechanism | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Investor-State Dispute Settlement (ISDS)** | Corporations sue host governments for policy changes affecting their profits |
| **Arbitration** | Disputes resolved in private, corporate-friendly forums |
| **Tax avoidance** | Transfer pricing, offshoring profits, and other legal means to avoid local taxation |
| **Regulatory capture** | Corporations influence host government regulations |
| **Contract opacity** | Concession agreements are confidential or not publicly available |

Studies show "clear continuity between colonial legal instruments—such as terra nullius, concession laws, and sovereignty-denying codes—and modern corporate legal strategies." Case studies of Chevron in Ecuador, Vedanta in Zambia, and Shell in Nigeria illustrate how multinational corporations use lawfare to consolidate control, suppress opposition, and neutralize local legal systems.

### 4.3 The EU's Corporate Due Diligence Directive

The EU's Corporate Sustainability Due Diligence Directive (CSDDD), approved in 2024, attempts to regulate corporate behavior in global supply chains. However, critics argue the Directive "has a limited value for Africans because it cannot stop corporate human rights abuses and economic exploitation in Africa."

**The critique**:
- The Directive applies only to "very large" companies (1,000+ employees, EUR 450M+ turnover)
- This excludes less than 1% of EU corporations
- The proposed 2025 Omnibus amendment further dilutes corporate responsibility
- From a Third World perspective, the Directive "entrenches neocolonial norms in the Business and Human Rights field"

As one scholar argues, "it is futile for Third World Peoples to look to international law, given its capitalist history and the growing EU dominance." Instead, African states must "look inward to create subsidiary norms that challenge and resist neocolonialism."

## Part 5: The 2026 Moment — The Return of Corporate Colonialism

### 5.1 Rubio's "Munich Manifesto"

On February 14, 2026, U.S. Secretary of State Marco Rubio delivered an address at the Munich Security Conference that was described by analysts as a manifesto for recolonization—a blueprint to restore Western dominance by any means necessary.

**Key passages**:
> "For five centuries, before the end of the Second World War, the West had been expanding—its missionaries, its pilgrims, its soldiers, and its explorers pouring out from its shores to cross oceans, settle new continents, and build vast empires extending out across the globe. But in 1945, for the first time since the age of Columbus, it was contracting…The great Western empires had entered into terminal decline, accelerated by godless communist revolutions and by anti-colonial uprisings that would transform the world."

**The reaction**: As French analyst Arnaud Bertrand observed, "It is one of the most revisionist and imperialist speeches I've ever seen a senior American official make…He wants to restore the building of 'vast empires extending across the globe' and blames 'anti-colonial uprisings' for what they did to 'the great Western empires.'"

### 5.2 The Corporate Recolonization Model

Rubio's rhetoric was not theoretical—it was being operationalized:

| Action | Corporate Logic |
|--------|-----------------|
| **Maduro kidnapping (Venezuela)** | "With Maduro imprisoned, US companies can move in to take over the oilfields" |
| **Iran war and Strait of Hormuz** | "What we want to do is get such massive oil reserves in Iran out of the hands of terrorists…Ultimately, we're not going to have to worry about these issues in the Strait of Hormuz" |
| **South Africa land policy** | US President Trump and Elon Musk demanded that South Africa abandon its insistence that corporations include 30% ownership for the majority |

As one analyst noted, this is "corporate empire in action"—a model "eerily reminiscent of the old East India Company."

### 5.3 The East India Company Parallel

The Trump administration's foreign policy was described as functioning "like a family enterprise—a 'Board of Peace' in Gaza, a crypto council stacked with relatives—centralizing decisions and bypassing traditional statecraft." The logic was "destabilization and resource capture"—the same playbook used by the East India Company in the 18th century.

## Part 6: Resistance and the African Response

### 6.1 Historical Resistance

Resistance to corporate colonialism has a long history. Alice Kinloch, a South African pan-Africanist born in 1863, established the African Association in 1897 and organized the first Pan African Conference in 1900. She wrote:

> "The handsome dividends that a certain company pays are earned at the price of blood and souls of…black men. Shareholders may be in happy ignorance of this, so we would remind them that there are several thousands of fellow-men kept under lock and key for their sole benefit, and that the gems on their wives' hands, and the finery bought by their 'profits' are, to 'seeing' eyes, bespattered with human gore."

### 6.2 The Binding Treaty on Business and Human Rights

In 2014, the UN Human Rights Council began negotiating "an international legally binding instrument to regulate, in international human rights law, the activities of transnational corporations and other business enterprises." The treaty process has been led from the outset by the Global South.

**Current status (2026)**: Efforts toward corporate accountability "are backsliding everywhere. Both the US and the EU are rolling back what little controls they had in place to regulate the actions of corporations." Countries of the Global South are being put under pressure to ease regulations to facilitate the second "scramble for Africa" under the banner of a "green transition."

### 6.3 Africanization of Business and Human Rights

Scholars argue that Africa must "look inward to decolonize Eurocentric norms that facilitate the exploitation of African natural resources." The African Union must take the lead in "developing policy and treaty norms that Africanize BHR."

**The conclusion**: "No one is coming to save Africa; Africa must save itself from neocolonial norms that are inimical to the socio-economic interests of its people."
