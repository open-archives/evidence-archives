## Executive Summary

Africa remains the clearest living laboratory of neocolonial dynamics. Formal independence in the mid-20th century did not end external extraction—it merely evolved the methods. Today, no single power "runs" the continent. Instead, a competitive, overlapping system of influence persists through sovereign debt, infrastructure monopolies, resource capture, security pacts, and the co-optation of local elites.

This entry documents the transition from unilateral Western dominance to **competitive multi-polar extraction**. It maps the primary actors—China, France, the United States, and emerging secondary players like the Gulf States and Russia—and outlines the specific mechanisms each employs to secure access to the strategic minerals (cobalt, lithium, rare earths) that will define the 21st-century energy transition.

The fundamental pattern remains intact: local elites act as gatekeepers to resource wealth, while populations bear the debt burdens, environmental degradation, and loss of sovereignty. Colonialism did not end; it adapted.

## Part 1: China — Dominant Infrastructure & Resource Player

China has accelerated the shift away from Western dominance, establishing itself as the largest single creditor and most prolific infrastructure developer on the continent.

### 1.1 The Belt & Road Leverage
Through the Belt and Road Initiative (BRI), China has financed and constructed critical infrastructure—ports, railways, mines, and power grids—in nations like Angola, Zambia, Ethiopia, and Djibouti. 
- **The Mechanism**: Massive sovereign loans are often collateralized by future commodity exports (oil, copper, cobalt).
- **Dual-Use Potential**: Strategic ports (e.g., Djibouti) and digital infrastructure (Huawei networks, submarine cables) provide both commercial dominance and intelligence/military utility.

### 1.2 The "No Strings" Illusion
While Chinese state-backed capital is famously marketed as having "no strings attached" regarding domestic governance and human rights (a sharp contrast to Western conditionality), the terms of the debt are notoriously opaque. Deals are frequently negotiated directly with political elites, shielding the contracts from public or parliamentary scrutiny.

## Part 2: France — Persistent Post-Colonial Influence

France maintains a deep, structural grip on its former colonies, particularly in Francophone West and Central Africa, though this influence is facing unprecedented erosion.

### 2.1 The CFA Franc Zone
The CFA franc remains one of the most explicit remnants of colonial architecture in the modern world. It guarantees monetary control by tying African economies to Paris through reserve requirements and exchange rate pegs, effectively limiting the macroeconomic sovereignty of member states.

### 2.2 Security and Corporate Extraction
- **Military Presence**: Persistent basing and security pacts, justified under the banner of counter-terrorism (e.g., the Sahel operations).
- **Corporate Monopolies**: Deeply entrenched corporate extraction by French champions (Areva/Orano in uranium, TotalEnergies in oil, and Bolloré in logistics and port management).
- **The Backlash**: Recent military coups and widespread populist protests in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger signal a rapid collapse of French soft power and a rejection of the *Françafrique* model.

## Part 3: United States & Western Allies

Western influence relies heavily on financial institutions, normative rhetoric, and security architecture.

### 3.1 Debt and the IFIs
The legacy of the IMF and World Bank Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs) continues to shape African economies. Western influence is exercised through conditionality—tying debt relief or development loans to privatization, austerity, and open-market reforms that favor Western multinational corporations.

### 3.2 The Soft Power / Hard Power Matrix
- **Hard Power**: Security partnerships, AFRICOM drone bases, and counter-terrorism funding maintain the U.S. security umbrella across strategic chokepoints.
- **Soft Power**: Tech platforms, financial infrastructure, and an extensive network of NGOs project Western influence.
- **The Rhetorical Gap**: U.S. policy frequently blends democracy and governance rhetoric with selective sanctions, even as Western mining and oil majors (often acting as junior partners to Chinese scale in new deals) quietly secure resource access.

## Part 4: Secondary Actors and Mercenary Influence

The multipolar nature of modern extraction has invited new players into the scramble.

| Actor | Mechanisms of Influence |
|-------|-------------------------|
| **Gulf States (UAE, Saudi Arabia)** | Port acquisitions, massive agricultural land grabs, and military basing along the highly strategic Horn of Africa and Red Sea corridors. |
| **Russia (Africa Corps / Wagner)** | Deploying mercenary forces to fragile states in exchange for direct access to gold, diamonds, and critical minerals, coupled with aggressive disinformation campaigns. |
| **Turkey** | Expanding drone sales, construction contracts, and diplomatic footprint to secure regional leverage. |
| **India & Brazil** | Leveraging historical non-aligned ties for growing, though secondary, trade and agricultural investment. |

## Part 5: The Core Pattern and Human Cost

Neocolonialism today is characterized by **competitive multi-polar extraction** rather than old-school European monopoly.

### 5.1 The Elite Gatekeeper Model
African populations rarely see the benefits of the new "Scramble for Africa." Local political and military elites act as gatekeepers, trading national resources for personal kickbacks, regime security, or prestige infrastructure. Debt servicing frequently exceeds new aid or investment, trapping states in a permanent liquidity crisis.

### 5.2 The Green Transition Catalyst
The global push for green energy has intensified the scramble. Strategic minerals—such as cobalt in the DRC, lithium in Zimbabwe, and rare earths across the continent—are the new rubber and gold. 

### 5.3 The Human Toll
Despite staggering resource wealth, the human cost remains devastating:
- Persistent structural poverty and inequality
- Geopolitical instability fueled by external arms and mercenary support
- Massive migration pressures
- The deliberate use of divide-and-rule tactics (exploiting ethnic or religious fault lines) combined with captured media to sustain the extractive system

Africa possesses agency—seen in the pushback against French influence, the renegotiation of BRI debt, and attempts at strategic diversification—but the structural asymmetries of the global financial system remain formidably steep.
