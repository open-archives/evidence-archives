## Part 1: The Historical Foundation — Extraction, Proxy Wars, and the Resource Curse

---

### Executive Summary

The current multi-player competition for influence in Africa cannot be understood without grasping the deep historical foundations laid by colonialism, Cold War proxy battles, and the post-independence resource curse. The arbitrary borders drawn at the Berlin Conference (1884–85), the extractive economies established by concession companies, the Cold War use of African states as battlegrounds for superpower rivalry, and the structural adjustment programs of the 1980s–90s all created the conditions for the contemporary scramble.

This entry traces the arc of Africa's integration into the global political economy: from colonial extraction (rubber, ivory, minerals, human labor), through the post-independence continuity of elite extraction, through Cold War proxy warfare (Angola, Mozambique, Ethiopia, Somalia), through the debt trap of IMF/World Bank structural adjustment, to the pre-BRI landscape of Chinese engagement (1950s–2000). The extractive logic has remained constant across four centuries—the players have changed, but the structural patterns have shown significant continuity.

The entry concludes with a framework for understanding Africa not as a passive victim of external forces, but as a complex arena where state and non-state actors negotiate, resist, and shape outcomes. The foundation laid here is essential for understanding the multi-player competition documented in Parts 2–8.

## Part 1.1: The Scramble for Africa — Berlin Conference and Arbitrary Borders

### 1.1.1 The Berlin Conference (1884–85)

The Berlin Conference, convened by German Chancellor Otto von Bismarck, brought together representatives of 14 European nations and the United States to regulate European colonization and trade in Africa. The conference established the principle that European powers could claim African territories only if they effectively occupied them and notified other powers of their claims.

**Key outcomes**:
- The Congo Free State was recognized as the personal property of King Leopold II of Belgium
- The "principle of effective occupation" was established
- Africa was carved into spheres of influence with no regard for existing ethnic, linguistic, or cultural boundaries
- No African representatives were present

**The legacy**: The arbitrary borders drawn at Berlin continue to shape African political geography. They have been a source of conflict, instability, and state fragility for over a century.

### 1.1.2 Arbitrary Borders and Their Consequences

| Consequence | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **Ethnic divisions** | Ethnic groups were split across multiple states (e.g., the Hutu/Tutsi division across Rwanda, Burundi, DRC, Uganda) |
| **Artificial states** | States were created with little to no historical precedent; governance structures were imposed from outside |
| **Resource enclaves** | Resources were extracted through enclave economies with minimal local benefit |
| **Weak institutions** | Colonial administrations did not develop strong state institutions; post-independence states inherited hollow structures |
| **Conflict** | Artificial borders have been a source of conflict, secessionist movements, and civil war |

## Part 1.2: Colonial Extraction — Rubber, Ivory, Minerals, and Human Labor

### 1.2.1 The Concession Company Model

The chartered companies documented in `EL-COL-11-CORPORATE-COLONIALISM-2026` were the primary vehicles of colonial extraction in Africa. They operated on a simple model:

**Extraction → Export → Profit → Metropolitan Enrichment**

| Company | Territory | Primary Extraction |
|---------|-----------|-------------------|
| **British South Africa Company** | Southern Rhodesia, Northern Rhodesia, Nyasaland | Gold, copper, ivory |
| **Royal Niger Company** | Nigeria | Palm oil, rubber, ivory |
| **Imperial British East African Company** | Kenya, Uganda | Ivory, rubber, land |
| **German East Africa Company** | Tanzania | Rubber, ivory, sisal |
| **Companhia de Moçambique** | Mozambique | Labor, sugar, tea, minerals |

### 1.2.2 The Extraction Logic

The extraction logic of colonial Africa was consistent: resources flowed out; minimal value returned.

**The mechanism**:
1. Concession companies were granted exclusive rights over vast territories
2. Local populations were forced into labor (forced labor, taxation as labor coercion, the *chibaro* system)
3. Resources were extracted with no local processing or value addition
4. Profits flowed to shareholders in metropolitan countries
5. Infrastructure was built only to facilitate extraction (ports, railways from mines to coast)

**The human cost**: The extraction of resources was accompanied by the extraction of human labor. Forced labor, taxation as a means of compelling labor, and the use of violence to maintain order were standard features of colonial extraction.

### 1.2.3 The Resource Curse Origins

The "resource curse"—the paradox that countries with abundant natural resources tend to have worse economic outcomes—has documented roots in colonial extraction.

| Dimension | Colonial Feature | Post-Independence Legacy |
|-----------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Extractive economies** | Colonies were structured as enclave economies | Post-independence states inherited the same structure |
| **Elite capture** | Colonial administrations worked through local elites | Post-independence elites replicated the extraction model |
| **Weak institutions** | Colonial states were designed to extract, not develop | Weak state capacity persists |
| **Dependency** | Economies dependent on commodity exports | Commodity dependence persists |
| **Infrastructure** | Infrastructure for extraction only | Infrastructure gaps persist |

## Part 1.3: Independence and Continuity — The Persistence of Extraction

### 1.3.1 Independence (1957–1975)

Between 1957 (Ghana) and 1975 (Angola, Mozambique, Cape Verde), virtually all of Africa gained formal independence from colonial rule. The transition was complex and varied:

- Some transitions were negotiated (Ghana, Nigeria)
- Some followed violent liberation struggles (Angola, Mozambique, Algeria)
- Some were marked by settler resistance (Algeria, Southern Rhodesia)

### 1.3.2 The Continuity of Elite Extraction

Formal independence did not end extraction. It changed the players.

| Dimension | Pre-Independence | Post-Independence |
|-----------|------------------|-------------------|
| **Extractor** | Colonial companies and colonial states | Post-independence elites and multinational corporations |
| **Structure** | Enclave extraction | Enclave extraction |
| **Beneficiaries** | Metropolitan shareholders | Elites, multinational corporations, foreign governments |
| **Local benefit** | Minimal | Minimal |

**The mechanism**: Post-independence elites, often educated in metropolitan countries, inherited the colonial state structure. They controlled access to resources and used that control to enrich themselves and consolidate power. The extraction model continued—but the beneficiaries were now national elites as well as foreign corporations.

### 1.3.3 The "Big Man" System

The post-independence African state was often captured by a "Big Man"—a leader who controlled access to state resources and used that control to maintain power.

**Features of the Big Man system**:
- Control over access to resources (minerals, agriculture, permits, contracts)
- Patronage networks that reward loyalty
- Weak institutions; rule of law subordinated to personal power
- Violence as a tool of control
- External patrons who support the leader in exchange for access

This system was not created by independence. It was inherited from colonial structures. But it was reinforced and perpetuated by the post-independence elite.

## Part 1.4: Cold War Proxy Battles — Africa as a Superpower Battleground

### 1.4.1 The Cold War Framework

The Cold War transformed Africa into a battleground for U.S.-Soviet rivalry. Superpowers supported clients, funded proxy wars, and used African states as pawns in a global chess game.

| Proxy War | Years | Players | Outcome |
|-----------|-------|---------|---------|
| **Angolan Civil War** | 1975–2002 | MPLA (Soviet/Cuban support) vs. UNITA (U.S./South African support) | MPLA victory; continued instability |
| **Mozambique Civil War** | 1977–1992 | FRELIMO (Soviet support) vs. RENAMO (U.S./South African support) | FRELIMO victory; devastating war |
| **Ethiopia-Somalia** | 1977–1978 | Ethiopia (Soviet/Cuban support) vs. Somalia (U.S. support) | Ethiopian victory; Somalia's collapse |
| **DRC (Congo) Crisis** | 1960–1965 | Lumumba (Soviet support) vs. Mobutu (U.S. support) | Mobutu's victory; long dictatorship |

### 1.4.2 The Consequences of Proxy Warfare

The Cold War proxy battles had documented consequences for African states:

| Consequence | Description |
|-------------|-------------|
| **State destruction** | Civil wars destroyed infrastructure, state capacity, and social cohesion |
| **Militarization** | Large armies, armed militias, and the proliferation of small arms |
| **Refugee flows** | Millions displaced across borders |
| **Elite capture** | State resources diverted to military spending |
| **Authoritarian consolidation** | Superpowers supported dictators who aligned with them |
| **Regional destabilization** | Cross-border conflicts and regional conflict complexes |

### 1.4.3 Mobutu's Zaire: The Model of the Post-Colonial Extraction State

The regime of Mobutu Sese Seko in Zaire (now DRC) exemplifies the post-colonial extraction state:

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Resource control** | Control over copper, cobalt, diamonds, and other resources |
| **Elite capture** | State resources diverted to the president, his family, and his network |
| **Weak institutions** | State capacity eroded; private networks replaced public institutions |
| **External patrons** | U.S. support during the Cold War; provided military and economic aid |
| **Violence** | Coercion and violence to maintain control |
| **Extraction** | Resources extracted with minimal local benefit |

The Mobutu model was not unique. It was replicated across the continent.

## Part 1.5: Structural Adjustment — IMF/World Bank Neoliberal Reforms

### 1.5.1 The Debt Crisis

By the 1980s, many African states were in deep debt. The debt crisis was driven by:

- Oil price shocks (1970s)
- Borrowing for infrastructure and military spending
- Falling commodity prices
- Interest rate hikes (U.S. Federal Reserve)
- Corruption and elite capture of loans

### 1.5.2 Structural Adjustment Programs

The IMF and World Bank responded with **Structural Adjustment Programs (SAPs)** —a package of neoliberal reforms imposed on borrowing countries.

| Policy | Purpose | Effect |
|--------|---------|--------|
| **Privatization** | Selling state-owned enterprises | Transfer of assets to private (often foreign) hands |
| **Austerity** | Reducing government spending | Cuts to healthcare, education, and social services |
| **Trade liberalization** | Removing tariffs and trade barriers | Domestic industries destroyed by cheaper imports |
| **Deregulation** | Reducing government regulation of markets | Weakened labor and environmental protections |
| **Currency devaluation** | Making exports cheaper | Reduced purchasing power for imports |

### 1.5.3 The Human Cost of Structural Adjustment

The human cost of SAPs was significant:

| Impact | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Health** | Cuts to healthcare increased mortality and disease |
| **Education** | Cuts to education reduced enrollment and literacy |
| **Poverty** | Increased poverty and inequality |
| **Emigration** | Millions left to find work elsewhere |
| **State capacity** | Weak states became weaker; unable to provide basic services |

### 1.5.4 The Debt Trap

The structural adjustment reforms did not solve the debt problem. They shifted the burden from foreign creditors to domestic populations.

**The debt trap mechanism**:
1. African governments borrowed from Western banks and institutions
2. Interest rate hikes made debt unaffordable
3. IMF/World Bank imposed structural adjustment conditions
4. Governments cut spending on social services
5. Debt persisted; new borrowing was needed to service old debt
6. The cycle continued

## Part 1.6: The Pre-BRI Landscape — China's Early Engagement (1950s–2000)

### 1.6.1 Early Chinese Engagement

China's engagement with Africa did not begin with the BRI in 2013. It has documented roots.

**Key phases**:
- **1950s–1960s**: China supported African liberation movements; provided symbolic and some material support
- **1970s–1980s**: China's engagement was ideological; Chinese aid went to states aligned with Beijing (e.g., Tanzania, Zambia)
- **1990s**: China's engagement shifted toward economic interests; trade and investment grew
- **2000s**: China's engagement accelerated dramatically; resource extraction became central

### 1.6.2 The TAZARA Railway (1970–1976)

The Tanzania-Zambia Railway (TAZARA) was a significant project of Chinese engagement in Africa. It was built by Chinese firms and financed by Chinese loans (interest-free, with generous repayment terms). The railway opened up landlocked Zambia's copper exports.

**Significance**: TAZARA demonstrated China's capacity for infrastructure delivery in Africa. It established a model for future engagement: loans + Chinese contractors + infrastructure + resource access.

### 1.6.3 The Pre-BRI Trade and Investment Landscape

| Metric | 1990 | 2000 |
|--------|------|------|
| China-Africa trade | ~$1 billion | ~$10 billion |
| Chinese investment in Africa | Minimal | Growing but limited |
| Chinese infrastructure in Africa | TAZARA; limited other projects | Growing pipeline |

The BRI, launched in 2013, was not a departure from this trajectory—it was an acceleration and institutionalization.

## Part 1.7: The Framework — Africa as Arena, Not Victim

### 1.7.1 Africa as Arena

The narrative of Africa as a passive victim of external powers does not fully account for the evidence. African states and elites have agency. They negotiate, resist, and shape outcomes.

**Evidence of agency**:
- Elite preferences: African leaders choose partners based on their interests
- Diversification: African states build portfolios of partners
- Regional blocs: AU, ECOWAS, SADC coordinate positions
- Negotiation: Some states negotiate better terms than others
- Pushback: Debt renegotiations, policy shifts, and resistance

### 1.7.2 Governance Variable

Outcomes vary significantly based on the strength of domestic institutions:

| State Type | Examples | Negotiation Outcomes |
|------------|----------|---------------------|
| **Strong governance** | Ethiopia, Rwanda, Ghana | Better terms, more favorable deals |
| **Weak governance** | DRC, Chad, Mali | More vulnerable to asymmetric deals |
| **Fragile state** | Somalia, South Sudan | Minimal control over external actors |

### 1.7.3 The Continuum Framework

The current situation is not formal colonialism, but it sits on a continuum:

```
Formal Colonialism (Direct Rule)
        ↓
Neocolonial Economic Leverage (Debt, Resources, Infrastructure)
        ↓
Competitive Multipolarity (Multiple Powers Competing for Influence)
```
