## Part 2: Occupancy Guarantee Mechanisms

### Contract Clauses
| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Lock-Up Quota** | State must keep 80-100% beds filled |
| **Low-Crime Tax** | Payment for unused beds when crime drops |
| **Per-Diem Rate** | Daily charge per inmate regardless of occupancy |
| **Penalty Clause** | State pays for empty capacity |

### Documented Case Studies
| State | Contract | Outcome |
|-------|----------|---------|
| **Colorado** | 3 private prison contracts | Paid **$2 million extra** despite crime dropping |
| **Arizona** | 3 contracts with 100% quota | Forced full occupancy regardless of demand |
| **Illinois** | 20-year Lake Erie contract | Reduced safety spending, overcrowding increased |
| **Utah** | MTC Kingman contract | Bed guarantee provisions in budget |
| **Florida** | 65% of contracts analyzed | Taxpayer penalties when crime decreased |

### Source: In the Public Interest (2013)
> **"Two-thirds of private prison contracts include 'lockup quotas' requiring states to fill beds or pay penalties."**

## Part 5: Incentive Structure

### Profit Model
Taxpayer Dollars (Federal/State)
↓
Contracts with Private Prisons
↓
Occupancy Guarantees (80-100% beds filled)
↓
Lobbying ($2M+/year industry-wide)
↓
Stricter Sentencing Laws → More Prisoners
↓
Higher Revenue → Higher Profits
↓
Back to Top (Campaign Donations)

### Perverse Incentives
| Situation | Company Profit | Public Safety |
|-----------|----------------|---------------|
| **Crime Rises** | ✓ More prisoners | ✗ Worse safety |
| **Crime Falls** | ✓ Penalty payments | ✓ Better safety |
| **Rehabilitation** | ✗ Fewer prisoners | ✓ Better outcomes |
| **Sentence Length ↑** | ✓ Longer revenue | ✗ More incarceration |
