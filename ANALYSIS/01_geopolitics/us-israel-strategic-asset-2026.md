## Executive Summary

Israel functions as a forward military base, intelligence partner, and proxy actor for U.S. strategic interests in the Middle East. This relationship is grounded in a cold strategic calculation that predates AIPAC's influence. As then-Senator Joe Biden stated in 1986: *"Without Israel, the United States would have to create an Israel to protect its interests in the region."*

However, the relationship is not purely instrumental. It also reflects shared threats, domestic political dynamics, genuine values alignment, and real costs and risks. This entry documents both the strategic rationale and its limits, acknowledging that Israel acts with independent agency and that U.S.-Israel interests diverge on key issues.

## Part 3: Mutual Interests and Shared Threats

The U.S.-Israel relationship is not purely a one-way proxy arrangement. Both countries face threats from Iran and its proxies, and Israel provides unique capabilities that benefit the U.S. directly.

| Shared Threat | How Both Benefit |
|---------------|------------------|
| **Iran's nuclear program** | Israel shares intelligence and develops missile defense systems |
| **Hezbollah / Hamas / PIJ** | Both see these groups as destabilizing forces |
| **Jihadist terrorism** | Joint counterterrorism cooperation |
| **Tech innovation** | Israel co-develops AI, drone, and cyber systems used by the U.S. |
| **Intel on Iranian nuclear sites** | Israel provides unique human and technical intelligence |

### The Qualitative Military Edge

U.S. policy has long committed to maintaining Israel's "Qualitative Military Edge" (QME) in the region. This means Israel receives advanced weapons systems (F-35s, bunker-busters, missile defense) that other regional allies do not. The QME policy is part of the formal U.S.-Israel relationship, codified in U.S. law since 2008.

**The QME is not about Israel being a proxy. It is about Israel being a uniquely capable ally.**

## Part 6: Divergences — Where U.S. and Israel Don't Align

The relationship is not total alignment. Key divergences exist.

| Divergence | U.S. Position | Israeli Position |
|------------|---------------|------------------|
| **Settlements** | U.S. has called them an obstacle to peace | Israel continues expansion |
| **Iran nuclear deal (2015)** | U.S. supported JCPOA | Israel opposed it |
| **Two-state solution** | U.S. officially supports (rhetorically) | Israel's government has moved away from it |
| **Annexation** | U.S. has warned against it | Some Israeli leaders have pursued it |

**Other historical friction points:**
- USS *Liberty* incident (1967) — Israeli attack on U.S. naval vessel
- Jonathan Pollard spy case (1985) — Israeli espionage against the U.S.
- Repeated U.S. arms sales to Arab states (Saudi, UAE) — Israel has often opposed

**Bottom line:** Israel is not a puppet. It acts independently — sometimes against U.S. preferences — but the strategic overlap remains strong.

## Part 10: The Wesley Clark Context — Clarifying the "Seven Countries" Claim

The Wesley Clark memo is often cited as evidence of a U.S.-Israel plan to destroy seven countries. This is an oversimplification.

| Claim | Reality |
|-------|---------|
| "The U.S. planned to take out seven countries" | Clark described a post-9/11 Pentagon memo targeting terror-sponsoring regimes |
| "Israel drove the plan" | Israel was one factor, but the plan reflected broader U.S. strategic interests (oil, anti-terrorism, anti-Iran) |
| "It was a neat, Israel-driven plan" | It was messier and more complex; multiple factors drove each intervention |

**Bottom line:** Israel was a beneficiary of the policy, not the author.
